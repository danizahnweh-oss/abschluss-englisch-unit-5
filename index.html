Recap The four phases of globalisation

Trade between distant peoples is ancient (Silk Road merchants, 2nd century BC). But modern globalisation is usually split into four phases:

  • Globalisation 1.0 (mid-19th century → 1914): the Industrial Revolution, steamships and railways hugely expand worldwide trade.
  • Globalisation 2.0 (after WWII): the USA becomes the dominant power; rapid growth in technology, communication and finance ever more connects the world.
  • Globalisation 3.0 (around 2000): the internet transforms work and leisure; the middle classes in China and India grow fast, yet the richest 1% take about 60% of income growth.
  • Globalisation 4.0 (now): a phase of rapid technological, social and economic change – while some commentators argue we are instead entering deglobalisation.

Read Why English?

Roughly one in four people worldwide speak English, and about 750 million of them do not come from an English-speaking country. English owes its dominance to two things: Britain's colonial expansion (17th–19th centuries), which imposed the language on many countries, and the 20th-century rise of the USA as a political, business and cultural superpower – reinforced by Hollywood films and pop music. Today English is the world's main lingua franca for trade and communication.

Recap The container that runs the world

About 90% of the world's goods travel by sea, most of them in standardized steel containers. In 1956 the American entrepreneur Malcolm McLean created the standard container. Because containers fit together “like lego blocks”, loading became mechanised, faster and far cheaper. The quantity of goods carried in containers soared from 102 million tons (1980) to about 1.83 billion tons (2017).

The system's weakness showed in 2021 when the giant ship Ever Given blocked the Suez Canal, through which about 12% of global shipping passes. The blockage cost an estimated $9.6 billion a day.

based on Anna Nagumey, in: The Conversation, 2021

Read Is globalisation doomed?

After the Cold War, many hoped the whole world would move towards liberal democracy and free markets – and that China's WTO membership (2001) would transform it into a Western-style economy. When that didn't happen, disappointment grew. Recent shocks (the pandemic, Russia's invasion of Ukraine) pushed countries towards strategic autonomy, reshoring and friend-shoring – a partial decoupling from global supply chains.

The author warns that deglobalisation risks lower productivity and higher prices. His key point: a healthy social contract that protects workers – not protectionism – is the better way to reduce the downsides of globalisation.

based on André Sapir, Bruegel website, 2022

Recap E-waste in Accra, Ghana

At the Agbogbloshie dump in Accra, up to 10,000 workers burn and break apart discarded electronics to recover valuable metals such as copper. It is one of the world's largest destinations for used electronic goods. The work brings burns, respiratory problems and chronic illness.

Around 50 million tons of e-waste are thrown away each year – a figure projected to double by 2050 – yet only about 20% is recycled appropriately. Much is sent, often illegally, from richer countries to Africa and Asia.

based on Peter Yeung, in: Bloomberg, 2019

Read Doughnut Economics

Economist Kate Raworth (2017) suggests replacing the old “growth for ever” model with a doughnut. The inner ring is the social foundation – the basics everyone needs (food, water, health, education, housing, energy, political voice). The outer ring is the ecological ceiling – limits we must not cross (climate change, ocean acidification, biodiversity loss, pollution).

The goal is to bring humanity into the “sweet spot” between the two rings: meeting everyone's needs within the planet's limits. Cities such as Amsterdam have adopted the model for public policy.

based on Robert Raymond, Shareable website, 2021 · Kate Raworth (2017)

Recap Can English remain the world's favourite language?

About 1.5 billion people speak English, but fewer than 400 million as a first language. Because so many use it as a second or third language, hybrid forms are spreading: Hinglish (Hindi-English), Benglish (Bengali-English), Tanglish (Tamil-English) and, in the US, Spanglish. Meanwhile, real-time translation technology is improving fast, and the rise of China makes Mandarin more attractive. Even so, English is still widely seen as a “passport to success”.

based on Robin Lustig, BBC website, 2018

Read Decolonising the mind – Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o

The Kenyan writer Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o describes how, in colonial Kenya, English became the measure of intelligence and the key to education and status, while African languages were devalued. Children were punished for speaking their mother tongue at school. For Ngũgĩ, language is a carrier of culture: forcing a foreign language onto people alienates them from their own world. Since 1978 he has written his creative work in Gikuyu.

based on Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o, Decolonising the Mind, 1986

Recap Broadsheet vs tabloid

  • Broadsheet (quality) newspapers: clear distinction between news and opinion, objective style, arguments backed by dates, facts, examples and quotes.
  • Tabloids: sensationalised headlines, mixing of facts and opinion, vivid descriptions and exaggeration.

When you analyse a non-fictional text, look at: text type, author's intention, register, structure (headline, introduction, body, conclusion), the line of argument, and the use of facts and figures.
